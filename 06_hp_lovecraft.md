# H.P. Lovecraft (1890-1937)

**Nationality:** American
**Genre:** Cosmic Horror, Weird Fiction
**Known For:** Creating the Cthulhu Mythos and founding the cosmic horror genre

---

## Life Experience

Howard Phillips Lovecraft was born on August 20, 1890, in Providence, Rhode Island. His father Winfield, a traveling salesman, suffered a psychotic episode (likely from untreated syphilis) in 1893 and was institutionalized at Butler Hospital, where he died in 1898. His mother Susie was increasingly overprotective and psychologically smothering — reportedly telling young Howard he was "hideous" and discouraging him from playing with other children.

His grandfather Whipple Phillips was the major positive influence, encouraging his reading and exposing him to weird fiction. When Whipple died in 1904 and the family lost their home, 13-year-old Lovecraft reportedly contemplated suicide.

### Education and Breakdown
A voracious autodidact reading by age 3-4, discovering Poe by age 8, deeply interested in chemistry and astronomy. He attended Hope Street High School but suffered a **nervous breakdown in 1908** that prevented him from attending Brown University — a failure that haunted him for life. He never received a high school diploma. From 1908 to ~1913, he lived as a virtual recluse.

### The New York Disaster (1924-1926)
In 1924, he married **Sonia Greene**, a Ukrainian Jewish immigrant and businesswoman. He moved to Brooklyn, but the New York period was disastrous — Sonia's business failed, he couldn't find work, lived in increasing poverty, and was horrified by the city's ethnic diversity. His already-present racism intensified dramatically. The marriage effectively dissolved; he returned to Providence in 1926.

### Providence Years (1926-1937)
Back in Providence, Lovecraft experienced his most productive literary period. He lived with his aunts in deepening poverty, earning tiny sums from pulp magazines (~1-1.5 cents per word) and ghostwriting. He often ate only canned beans and cheese, sometimes going hungry.

### The Correspondence Circle
Lovecraft wrote an estimated **87,500-100,000 letters** — some running 40-70 pages. His "Lovecraft Circle" of correspondents included Clark Ashton Smith, Robert E. Howard (creator of Conan), Robert Bloch (later wrote *Psycho*), August Derleth, Fritz Leiber, and many others. He was an extraordinarily generous mentor.

### Death
He suffered from chronic intestinal problems and malnutrition. Diagnosed with intestinal cancer and kidney disease, he died on March 15, 1937, at age 46. In 1977, fans erected a headstone inscribed **"I AM PROVIDENCE"** — from one of his letters.

## Writing Style

### Cosmic Horror / Cosmicism
Lovecraft's central innovation: humanity is utterly insignificant in a vast, indifferent universe populated by incomprehensibly powerful entities. Unlike traditional horror (good vs. evil), Lovecraftian horror stems from realizing that **good and evil are human constructs with no cosmic relevance**. The universe is not hostile — it is simply uncaring.

### Key Techniques
- **Forbidden knowledge:** Characters who investigate too deeply are invariably destroyed or driven mad. Knowledge itself is the threat.
- **Unreliable first-person narrators:** Typically educated scholars who insist on their sanity while describing the indescribable
- **Dread through implication:** Horrors barely glimpsed, partially described, or left to imagination. "I dare not describe," "words cannot convey" — the technique of cosmic indescribability
- **Archaic, ornate prose:** Deliberately modeled on 18th-century writers. Long sentences, Latinate vocabulary, heavily adjectival. Words like "eldritch," "cyclopean," "gibbous," "squamous" recur throughout
- **Non-linear revelation:** Narrators piece together truth from diary entries, newspaper clippings, scholarly research, and inherited documents
- **Scientific framing:** Horrors described with quasi-scientific precision — biological surveys, spectrometer analysis — making the impossible more disturbing
- **Hereditary degeneration:** Families tainted by interbreeding with non-human entities — reflecting his personal anxieties about racial "purity"

### The Cthulhu Mythos
A shared pseudo-mythology including:
- **Great Old Ones:** Cthulhu (sleeping in sunken R'lyeh), Nyarlathotep, Yog-Sothoth, Azathoth
- **Forbidden texts:** The Necronomicon, Pnakotic Manuscripts, Book of Eibon
- **Fictional New England:** Arkham (home of Miskatonic University), Dunwich, Innsmouth, Kingsport
- **Alien races:** Elder Things, Mi-Go, Deep Ones, Shoggoths, Great Race of Yith

Lovecraft intended this as a loose, shared backdrop — encouraging other writers to borrow and add to it.

## Speaking & Communication Style

- **"An old gentleman from Providence"** — self-consciously cultivated an 18th-century persona even when young. Archaic mannerisms, colonial architectural preferences, 18th-century spellings ("shew," "colour," "publick")
- **Extraordinarily prolific letter writer** — ~100,000 letters; some are essentially essays covering philosophy, science, history, literature, architecture
- **Formal even in casual correspondence** — signed letters "Yr most Obt Servt"; used "doubtless," "verily," "thereof" naturally
- **Encyclopedic autodidact** — deep knowledge of astronomy, chemistry, history, literature, philosophy, anthropology
- **Generous mentor** — selfless in offering detailed criticism and encouragement to younger writers
- **Dark humor** — funnier than his reputation suggests; self-deprecating wit
- **Virulently racist** — deeply racist, antisemitic, and xenophobic views, extreme even by his era's standards. Used racial slurs casually; wrote viciously racist poetry. Some evidence of moderate softening in his final years, but he never fully abandoned these views. His racism permeates his fiction.

## Representative Works

1. **"The Call of Cthulhu"** (1928) — Foundational text of the Cthulhu Mythos. Documentary investigation revealing Cthulhu's existence. Famous opening: "The most merciful thing in the world, I think, is the inability of the human mind to correlate all its contents."
2. **"At the Mountains of Madness"** (1936) — Antarctic expedition discovers ruins of an alien city built by Elder Things billions of years ago. Often considered his masterpiece of world-building.
3. **"The Shadow over Innsmouth"** (1936) — Decaying seaport whose residents have interbred with amphibious Deep Ones. Terrifying chase sequence; devastating final revelation. The only Lovecraft story published as a standalone book in his lifetime (~400 copies).
4. **"The Colour Out of Space"** (1927) — Lovecraft considered it his best story. A meteorite containing an unknowable alien "colour" corrupts everything on a farm. Pure cosmic horror with no mythology or cults — just the absolutely unknowable intruding into ordinary life.
5. **"The Dunwich Horror"** (1929) — Product of a human-Yog-Sothoth union threatens to open the way for the Old Ones. One of his few stories where humans successfully fight back.
6. **"The Whisperer in Darkness"** (1931) — Epistolary tension and one of his most chilling reveals involving the alien Mi-Go
7. **"The Shadow Out of Time"** (1936) — Mind-swapping Great Race of Yith builds a library of all knowledge across all eras. The final revelation — finding one's own handwriting in ancient ruins — is one of his most effective endings.
8. **"The Rats in the Walls"** (1924) — Ancestral estate concealing centuries of monstrous family practices. One of his most tightly constructed early stories.

## Legacy & Influence

- **Founded cosmic horror as a genre** — distinct from Gothic, supernatural, or psychological horror. Grounded in the fundamental nature of the cosmos, its indifference, and human inadequacy.
- **"Lovecraftian" became an adjective** — vast alien intelligences, forbidden knowledge, madness, existential dread
- **The Cthulhu Mythos expanded by hundreds of writers** — Ramsey Campbell, Thomas Ligotti, Victor LaValle, and many others. One of the most enduring shared fictional universes.
- **Enormous influence on games:** *Call of Cthulhu* RPG (1981), *Bloodborne*, *Darkest Dungeon*, *Amnesia*, *Arkham Horror*
- **Film influence:** *Alien* (1979), *The Thing* (1982), and much of Guillermo del Toro's work draw heavily on Lovecraftian themes
- **Stephen King** called him "the twentieth century's greatest practitioner of the classic horror tale"
- **Posthumous fame far exceeding lifetime:** Published almost exclusively in pulp magazines; earned pittance; died in poverty. Now recognized as one of the most important American writers of the 20th century. Library of America published a Lovecraft volume in 2005.
- **Controversial legacy:** World Fantasy Award trophy (a Lovecraft bust since 1975) was redesigned in 2015 after protests about his racism. Modern writers like Victor LaValle (*The Ballad of Black Tom*) and Ruthanna Emrys directly confront and reclaim his mythology from its racist roots. His racism cannot be separated from his work — but his literary innovations remain powerful.
