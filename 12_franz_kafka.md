# Franz Kafka (1883-1924)

**Nationality:** Czech (Austro-Hungarian Empire), German-speaking Jewish
**Genre:** Absurdist Fiction, Existentialist Fiction, Modernism
**Known For:** Coining "Kafkaesque"; *The Metamorphosis*, *The Trial*, *The Castle*

---

## Life Experience

Franz Kafka was born on July 3, 1883, in Prague, then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. The eldest of six children (two younger brothers died in infancy; three sisters — Elli, Valli, and Ottla — all later perished in the Holocaust).

### Triple Marginality
Kafka occupied a peculiarly isolated position: a German speaker in a predominantly Czech city, a Jew in a Christian society, and an assimilated Jew among both Germans and traditional Jews. This triple marginality — never fully belonging to any group — permeates his work's themes of alienation and exclusion.

### The Father
The defining relationship of his life was with his father **Hermann Kafka**, a physically imposing, self-made businessman — domineering, loud, bullying, contemptuous of Franz's literary ambitions. In 1919, Kafka wrote the famous **Letter to His Father** (never delivered), a 45-page document: "My writing was all about you; all I did there, after all, was to bemoan what I could not bemoan upon your breast." The theme of arbitrary, incomprehensible authority in *The Trial* and *The Castle* originates here.

### Career
Obtained a law degree from Karl-Ferdinand University in Prague (1906). Joined the **Workers' Accident Insurance Institute** in 1908, where he worked until illness forced retirement in 1922. Contrary to the image of a miserable bureaucrat, he was respected and promoted. He dealt with workplace safety and industrial accident claims — giving him intimate knowledge of bureaucratic machinery and institutional dehumanization. He wrote in the evenings and at night.

### Three Engagements, Never Married
- **Felice Bauer** — twice engaged (1914, 1917); relationship conducted largely through letters (sometimes 2-3/day); he wanted her and simultaneously felt marriage would destroy his writing
- **Julie Wohryzek** — briefly engaged 1919
- **Milena Jesenska** — intense correspondence (1920-22); arguably his most emotionally profound relationship
- **Dora Diamant** — lived together in Berlin 1923-24; closest to domestic happiness, though he was gravely ill

### Death
Diagnosed with tuberculosis in August 1917. By 1924, it had spread to his larynx, making eating or speaking excruciatingly painful. He died on **June 3, 1924**, at age 40, at the Kierling sanatorium near Vienna. Immediate cause: starvation (tubercular larynx made swallowing impossible). He was correcting proofs of *A Hunger Artist* on his deathbed — a story about a man who starves himself.

### Max Brod and the Manuscripts
Before death, Kafka asked his closest friend **Max Brod** to burn all unpublished manuscripts. **Brod refused.** He published *The Trial* (1925), *The Castle* (1926), and *Amerika* (1927), along with stories, diaries, and letters. Had Brod honored Kafka's wishes, the world would have lost ~90% of his work. One of the most consequential editorial decisions in literary history.

## Writing Style

### The Kafkaesque Method
The hallmark: a radical **disjunction between content and tone**. The most monstrous, impossible events are narrated in calm, precise, almost legalistic prose. When Gregor Samsa wakes as a giant insect, the narrative notes the practical difficulties of getting out of bed with multiple legs. This deadpan treatment of the impossible creates a unique effect — the reader cannot dismiss the horror as fantasy because the prose insists on its reality.

### Key Characteristics
- **Bureaucratic nightmares:** Vast, impenetrable systems of authority — courts, castles, hierarchies — operating by rules the protagonist cannot understand or appeal. Not merely corrupt but ontologically opaque.
- **Alienation and powerlessness:** Protagonists are isolated, excluded, confused, unable to communicate. Not social alienation but an existential condition of being a conscious being in an incomprehensible world.
- **Precise, clear German prose:** Deliberately stripped of ornament, influenced by legal writing and admiration for Kleist and Flaubert. Grammatically precise, logically structured, absolutely lucid — which makes the impossible content all the more disturbing.
- **Guilt without crime:** Characters feel guilty without knowing what they've done (Josef K.), unable to prove their identity or right to exist (K. in *The Castle*), or transformed into something unrecognizable (Gregor Samsa). To exist is already to be guilty.
- **Dreamlike logic:** Events connected but not causally; spaces shift; time behaves erratically. Unlike surrealists, Kafka's dreamlike quality is claustrophobic and anxiety-producing — a trap, not a liberation.

## Speaking & Communication Style

- **Shy and self-deprecating** — quiet, reserved, almost apologetically diffident despite being tall and strikingly handsome. Extraordinarily good listener.
- **Brilliant in letters** — among the greatest in literary history. His correspondence with Felice Bauer (500+ letters in 5 years) documents a man simultaneously pursuing and fleeing intimacy. Letters to Milena Jesenska are more openly passionate and vulnerable.
- **Much funnier than his reputation:** Max Brod reported Kafka laughed so hard reading the first chapter of *The Trial* aloud that he couldn't continue. His humor is deadpan, absurdist, deeply black — comedy of a situation so terrible that laughter is the only sane response.
- **In almost constant psychological distress** — insomnia, headaches, anxiety, depression, paralyzing inadequacy. "I am nothing but literature and can and want to be nothing else."
- Self-aware and darkly witty even in the bleakest diary passages.

## Representative Works

1. **The Metamorphosis** (Die Verwandlung, 1915) — His most famous story. Gregor Samsa wakes as a giant insect; his family's response evolves from shock to disgust to exploitation to relief at his death. Parable of alienation, dark family comedy, meditation on what makes us human.
2. **The Trial** (Der Process, 1925, posthumous) — Josef K. arrested for an unspecified crime by an unidentified court. Spends the novel navigating an incomprehensible judicial system. Ends with his execution, killed "like a dog." One of the defining novels of the 20th century.
3. **The Castle** (Das Schloss, 1926, posthumous) — K. arrives in a village governed by an inaccessible castle. Claims to be summoned as a land surveyor; the castle neither confirms nor denies. His attempts at recognition are endlessly futile. Left unfinished.
4. **Amerika** (The Man Who Disappeared, 1927, posthumous) — First novel attempt; the lightest and most Dickensian. Sixteen-year-old Karl Rossmann's picaresque adventures in a fantasized, never-visited America.
5. **In the Penal Colony** (1919) — Horrifying story about an execution machine that carves the prisoner's sentence into his body. Parable of justice, punishment, and the aestheticization of violence.
6. **A Hunger Artist** (1922) — One of his last stories, written as tuberculosis destroyed his ability to eat. A professional faster who fasted not from discipline but because he "couldn't find the food I liked." A meditation on art, asceticism, and the inability to participate in life.
7. **The Judgment** (Das Urteil, 1912) — Breakthrough story written in a single night. Father rises up and condemns son to death by drowning; son immediately obeys. Dream logic and father-son violence at its purest.
8. **Letter to His Father** (1919, posthumous) — Not fiction, but one of his most important texts. 45 pages of anguished analysis of how his father's domineering personality shaped his own paralysis and guilt. Never delivered.

## Legacy & Influence

- **"Kafkaesque" entered dozens of languages** — simultaneously absurd, nightmarish, and bureaucratic. One of the very few literary adjectives (alongside "Orwellian" and "Dickensian") in common speech. Applied to everything from the DMV to totalitarian regimes.
- **Foundational to existentialism and absurdism:** Camus devoted a key essay to Kafka in *The Myth of Sisyphus*. Sartre engaged with his themes of freedom and bad faith.
- **Modernist icon:** Alongside Joyce, Proust, and Woolf — permanently expanded what fiction could do.
- **Vast literary influence:** Borges, Garcia Marquez, Beckett, Camus, Kundera, Murakami, Coetzee, Philip Roth, Pynchon, Bolano. Borges wrote that Kafka "creates his own precursors" — after reading Kafka, you see Kafkaesque elements in writers who preceded him.
- **Enormous political resonance:** For Eastern European writers under Soviet communism, *The Trial* and *The Castle* were not metaphors but descriptions of daily reality. The 1963 Liblice conference on Kafka in Czechoslovakia was a landmark in the cultural thaw leading to the Prague Spring.
- **The Brod Question:** The fact that Kafka's work exists at all — that Brod defied his dying wish — raises profound questions about authorial intention and literary property. One of literature's great ethical dilemmas.
- **Uniquely incomplete:** His central works are unfinished fragments — the lack of resolution mirrors the content. Josef K. never learns his crime; K. never reaches the castle; the reader never gets closure. The incompleteness makes interpretation inexhaustible.
