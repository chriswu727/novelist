# Fyodor Dostoevsky (1821-1881)

**Nationality:** Russian
**Genre:** Psychological Fiction, Philosophical Fiction
**Known For:** Father of the psychological novel; exploring the darkest depths of human consciousness

---

## Life Experience

Fyodor Mikhailovich Dostoevsky was born on November 11, 1821, in Moscow, at the Mariinsky Hospital for the Poor where his father worked as a physician. His mother died of tuberculosis in 1837 when Fyodor was fifteen. In 1839, his father was **murdered on his country estate** — reportedly killed by his own serfs who poured vodka down his throat until he suffocated, driven by his cruel treatment. This event profoundly marked young Dostoevsky; Freud later speculated that the patricide theme in *The Brothers Karamazov* was rooted in his complicated feelings about this.

He studied at the Nikolayev Military Engineering Institute, graduating in 1843 but quickly abandoning engineering for writing. His first novel *Poor Folk* (1846) was an immediate success — critic Vissarion Belinsky proclaimed a new Gogol.

### Mock Execution and Siberian Exile
In 1849, Dostoevsky was arrested as a member of the **Petrashevsky Circle** (intellectuals discussing utopian socialism). Sentenced to death, he was led to Semyonovsky Square on December 22, 1849 — dressed in execution shrouds, lined up before a firing squad. **At the very last moment**, a messenger arrived with a commutation from Tsar Nicholas I. The entire execution had been staged as psychological torture. One prisoner went permanently insane on the spot.

Dostoevsky spent **four years (1850-1854) in a katorga labor camp** in Omsk, Siberia, followed by years of compulsory military service. The experience shattered his liberal idealism and deepened his Orthodox Christian faith.

### Gambling Addiction
Throughout the 1860s, consumed by ruinous roulette addiction. Would gamble away every kopeck, pawn his wife's belongings, write desperate letters begging for advances. He famously dictated *The Gambler* in just **26 days** to meet a predatory contract deadline, employing young stenographer Anna Grigoryevna Snitkina — who became his second wife.

### Epilepsy
Suffered seizures throughout his adult life, sometimes describing an ecstatic moment of transcendent harmony in the seconds before a seizure. He gave this experience to Prince Myshkin in *The Idiot*.

### Two Marriages
- **Maria Dmitrievna Isaeva** (1857-1864) — married during Siberian exile; unhappy marriage; she died of tuberculosis
- **Anna Grigoryevna Snitkina** (1867-1881) — the stenographer from *The Gambler*; became the stabilizing force of his life, managing finances and negotiations

### Later Fame and Death
By the 1870s, Dostoevsky achieved enormous recognition. His speech at the Pushkin Monument unveiling (1880) was received with rapturous acclaim. He died on February 9, 1881, from a pulmonary hemorrhage. Tens of thousands attended his funeral.

## Writing Style

### Psychological Depth
Perhaps the supreme literary psychologist. Characters are raging battlegrounds of contradictory impulses — Raskolnikov is simultaneously driven by intellectual pride, compassion, self-loathing, megalomania, and yearning for redemption. Dostoevsky understood the irrational, self-destructive, and masochistic dimensions of human psychology **decades before Freud**.

### Polyphonic Novels
Identified by literary theorist Mikhail Bakhtin: a narrative structure where multiple characters carry **fully articulated, independent worldviews** not subordinated to a single authorial position. In *The Brothers Karamazov*, Ivan's atheist rebellion, Alyosha's faith, Dmitri's chaos, and Smerdyakov's nihilism all carry genuine philosophical weight. The reader is not told which is correct.

### Key Characteristics
- **Existential and philosophical core:** Does God exist? If God doesn't exist, is everything permitted? What is freedom? Can suffering be justified? These questions are the structural core, not incidental themes.
- **Intense emotional drama:** Almost operatic pitch — confession, humiliation, confrontation, breakdown. Characters weep, scream, faint, fall at each other's feet. Not melodrama but conviction that the most important moments are moments of extreme crisis.
- **Crime as moral experiment:** Murder functions not as plot device but as a test — what happens to a human soul that has crossed the ultimate boundary?
- **Faith vs. doubt:** Dostoevsky described himself as "a child of disbelief and doubt" whose hosanna "passed through a great furnace of doubt." His most powerful arguments against God are so strong many readers find them more convincing than the counter-arguments he intended.
- **Stream of consciousness elements:** Before the term existed — fractured thought, interior monologue, consciousness under extreme pressure. The Underground Man's spiraling, self-contradictory monologue anticipates modernism.
- **Dense urban settings:** St. Petersburg as character — cramped rooms, stinking stairways, taverns, oppressive summer heat mirroring psychological states.

## Speaking & Communication Style

- **Intensely passionate and often difficult** — magnetic and eloquent but argumentative, touchy, prone to dominating conversations with impassioned monologues
- **Fierce debater** — genuinely agitated during intellectual disagreements on religion, Russian identity, and politics
- **Volcanic emotional range in letters** — desperate and pleading to publishers; tender and self-lacerating to his wife (especially confessing gambling losses); grandiose promises of reform
- **Extraordinary public reader** — could hold audiences spellbound for hours; reportedly brought listeners to tears or fainting during the murder scene from *Crime and Punishment*
- **Deeply religious in later years** — Russian Orthodox Christianity and messianic Russian nationalism infused his public persona
- **The Pushkin Speech (1880)** — an ecstatic vision of Russian universality that brought the audience to tears and hysteria

## Representative Works

1. **Crime and Punishment** (1866) — Raskolnikov, a destitute former student, murders a pawnbroker to test his theory that extraordinary people are above moral law. Tracks his psychological disintegration and eventual redemption through Sonya's love. One of the most influential novels ever written.
2. **The Brothers Karamazov** (1880) — His final and greatest novel. The Karamazov family — debauched father, passionate Dmitri, intellectual Ivan, spiritual Alyosha, illegitimate Smerdyakov — and the father's murder. Contains the **Grand Inquisitor** chapter: Ivan imagines Christ returning to Seville during the Inquisition, told by the Grand Inquisitor that the Church has "corrected" his work by taking away human freedom. One of the most famous passages in all literature.
3. **Notes from Underground** (1864) — The Underground Man rages against rationalism, utilitarianism, and the idea that humans act in self-interest. Widely considered **the first existentialist novel**. Opening: "I am a sick man... I am a spiteful man."
4. **The Idiot** (1868) — Prince Myshkin, a "positively good man" modeled on Christ and Don Quixote, enters corrupt St. Petersburg society and is destroyed. Dostoevsky's most personal novel.
5. **Demons** (The Possessed, 1872) — Political novel based on the real Nechaev affair. Prophetic analysis of how ideological fanaticism leads to violence and totalitarianism. Camus called it one of the four or five greatest novels ever.
6. **The Gambler** (1866) — Drawn from his own addiction; the feverish irrationality of compulsion. Dictated in 26 days.
7. **The House of the Dead** (1861-62) — Fictionalized account of his Siberian prison camp. Remarkable humanization of convicts.
8. **Poor Folk** (1846) — Debut. Epistolary novel about a lowly clerk. Established his concern with the downtrodden.

## Legacy & Influence

- **Father of existentialism in literature:** *Notes from Underground* is routinely cited as the first existentialist novel. His exploration of freedom, choice, absurdity, and a world without God laid the groundwork for the entire existentialist tradition.
- **Influenced philosophy profoundly:**
  - **Nietzsche** called him "the only psychologist from whom I have anything to learn"
  - **Freud** wrote a famous essay analyzing his gambling and Oedipal themes
  - **Sartre** used Ivan Karamazov's dictum as a starting point for existentialist ethics
  - **Camus** acknowledged him as foundational
- **Pioneer of the psychological novel:** Before Dostoevsky, novels depicted psychology from the outside. He went inside — into the irrational, contradictory, self-destructive depths. Directly influenced Freud's theory of the unconscious.
- **Immense literary influence:** Kafka, Thomas Mann, Hesse, Woolf, Joyce, Faulkner, Ellison, O'Connor, McCarthy, Murakami all cite him
- **Political prophecy:** *Demons* is widely regarded as having prophesied the mechanisms of revolutionary terror and totalitarianism that would engulf Russia in the 20th century
- Alongside Tolstoy, represents the **pinnacle of 19th-century Russian literature**
