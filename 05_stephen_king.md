# Stephen King (1947-)

**Nationality:** American
**Genre:** Horror, Thriller, Suspense, Literary Fiction
**Known For:** "The King of Horror" — 350+ million copies sold, one of the most adapted authors in history

---

## Life Experience

Stephen Edwin King was born on September 21, 1947, in Portland, Maine. His father Donald, a merchant seaman, went out for cigarettes one evening when Stephen was two and never returned. His mother Ruth raised two boys alone in near-poverty, working a series of low-paying jobs. A formative childhood moment: discovering a box of his father's horror and sci-fi paperbacks in the attic, igniting his love of the genre.

King attended the University of Maine at Orono (BA in English, 1970), where he wrote a column called "King's Garbage Truck" and met his wife Tabitha Spruce. After graduation, he couldn't find a teaching position and worked in an industrial laundry before getting a job teaching English at Hampden Academy for $6,400/year. The family lived in a trailer; their phone was sometimes disconnected.

### The Breakthrough
King famously threw an early draft of *Carrie* in the trash. Tabitha retrieved it and encouraged him to finish. Doubleday accepted it in 1973 with a $2,500 advance — then the paperback rights sold for $400,000. He quit teaching and never looked back.

### Addiction and Recovery
By King's own account, he became an alcoholic in the late 1970s and added cocaine addiction by the early 1980s. He barely remembers writing *Cujo*. His family staged an intervention (~1987-88), confronting him with physical evidence — beer cans, cocaine paraphernalia, cough syrup bottles. He got sober around 1988 and has remained so since. He writes about this unflinchingly in *On Writing*.

### The 1999 Van Accident
On June 19, 1999, King was struck by a van while walking along a Maine road. He suffered a collapsed lung, multiple leg fractures, broken hip, scalp lacerations, and a chipped spine. He considered retirement but returned to writing, using the experience to fuel his work.

### Career Milestones
- Published as **Richard Bachman** (pseudonym) to test whether his success was talent or luck
- **The Dark Tower** series (1982-2004): his self-described magnum opus spanning 7 novels
- **On Writing** (2000): widely considered one of the best books about the writing process
- National Book Foundation Medal (2003), National Medal of Arts (2014), Edgar Grand Master Award (2015)
- 65+ novels, ~200 short stories, 350+ million copies sold worldwide

## Writing Style

### Ordinary People in Extraordinary Situations
King's signature: thoroughly ordinary, recognizable people dropped into escalating terror. He starts with the human, not the monster. A recovering alcoholic takes a job at an isolated hotel. Children in a small town face an ancient evil. The horror is effective because readers already know and care about these people.

### Prose Characteristics
- **Colloquial and accessible:** Distinctly American vernacular, conversational, deliberately un-literary. Profanity used freely; reflects how Americans actually speak.
- **Character-driven:** Invests significant page time establishing who people are before introducing the threat — their memories, marriages, childhood traumas, petty resentments. This is why his books tend to be long.
- **Small-town America:** Castle Rock, Derry, Jerusalem's Lot — interconnected fictional Maine towns forming a geography compared to Faulkner's Yoknapatawpha County
- **Pop culture saturation:** Brand names, TV shows, songs, movies ground the fiction in recognizable reality
- **Stream of consciousness:** Italicized interior monologue letting readers hear characters' unfiltered, panicked, self-contradicting thoughts

### "Story Archaeology"
King describes stories as fossils to be excavated, not invented. He starts with "What if?" and lets characters determine where the story goes. He is a "pantser" (writing by the seat of his pants) rather than a "plotter." This gives narratives an organic, surprising quality, though critics sometimes find his endings weaker.

### Work Habits
Writes every day including holidays. Daily goal: ~2,000 words. First draft should take no longer than "the length of a season" (~3 months). Sets manuscript aside 6+ weeks before revising. His formula: "2nd Draft = 1st Draft - 10%."

## Speaking & Communication Style

- **Un-celebrity-like:** Slightly rumpled, tall (6'4"), bespectacled guy from Maine. Still lives in Bangor in a Victorian house with a famous bat-and-spider gate.
- **Genuinely funny** in a dry, deadpan, sometimes dark way. Self-deprecating humor.
- **Says what he thinks** — blunt, direct. Publicly critical of Kubrick's *The Shining*. Calls out overrated writers and praises underappreciated ones.
- **Practical about craft:** Talks about writing like a carpenter talks about building — it's a craft requiring daily work, not inspiration. "Read a lot, write a lot, cut adverbs."
- **"The road to hell is paved with adverbs."**
- **Politically outspoken** — vocal progressive, critic of Trump, supports gun control and universal healthcare
- **Active on social media** — mixes writing advice, political commentary, movie recommendations, and Red Sox updates
- **Folksy Maine identity** — despite enormous wealth, retains deep working-class New England identification

## Representative Works

1. **Carrie** (1974) — Debut novel. Bullied telekinetic girl pushed past her breaking point at prom. Established core themes: social cruelty, darkness in ordinary communities.
2. **The Shining** (1977) — Recovering alcoholic at an isolated haunted hotel with his family. Deeply autobiographical treatment of alcoholism and the fear of becoming a monster to one's own family.
3. **The Stand** (1978/1990) — Post-apocalyptic epic (1,100+ pages). Superflu wipes out civilization; survivors choose between good and evil. Often cited as his most ambitious novel.
4. **IT** (1986) — The Losers' Club vs. Pennywise the Dancing Clown in Derry, Maine. 1,100+ pages alternating between childhood (1958) and adulthood (1985). His most expansive exploration of childhood, memory, and friendship.
5. **Misery** (1987) — Novelist held captive by his "number one fan." Metaphor for the writer-audience relationship and King's own addiction.
6. **The Dark Tower** series (1982-2004) — Seven-book epic following Roland Deschain, the last gunslinger. Blends Western, fantasy, horror, and sci-fi. King considers it his magnum opus.
7. **The Shawshank Redemption** (novella, 1982) — Andy Dufresne's wrongful imprisonment and escape. Adapted into the consistently highest-rated film ever. Demonstrates King's range beyond horror.
8. **11/22/63** (2011) — Time-travel novel about preventing the JFK assassination. Part sci-fi, part love story, part meditation on history. One of his best later works.

## Legacy & Influence

- **Made horror mainstream:** Before King, horror was a niche genre. He demonstrated it could be the vehicle for serious explorations of American life — addiction, domestic violence, class, childhood trauma.
- **Most adapted living author:** *Carrie*, *The Shining*, *Stand By Me*, *Misery*, *Shawshank*, *The Green Mile*, *IT* — the sheer volume is staggering
- **"Dollar Baby" program** — allowed film students to adapt his short stories for $1
- **Broke the literary/genre barrier** — gradually received mainstream recognition (National Book Foundation Medal, National Medal of Arts)
- **On Writing** is assigned in creative writing programs worldwide
- **Created iconic cultural images:** Pennywise, the Overlook Hotel, Cujo, Shawshank — embedded in collective consciousness
- **Influenced a generation:** Joe Hill (his son), and countless writers who cite King as the writer who made them want to write
- **Philanthropy:** He and Tabitha have donated tens of millions to libraries, schools, hospitals, and community organizations in Maine
