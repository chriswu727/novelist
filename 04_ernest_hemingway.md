# Ernest Hemingway (1899-1961)

**Nationality:** American
**Genre:** Literary Modernism, War Fiction
**Known For:** The Iceberg Theory, revolutionizing American prose with stripped-down modernist style

---

## Life Experience

Ernest Miller Hemingway was born on July 21, 1899, in Oak Park, Illinois. His father Clarence was a physician; his mother Grace was a musician. Summers at Walloon Lake in northern Michigan instilled a lifelong love of the outdoors — fishing, hunting, camping — which became the backdrop for his Nick Adams stories.

After high school, he worked as a cub reporter at the *Kansas City Star*, where the terse, direct journalistic style shaped his literary voice. In 1918, he volunteered as a Red Cross ambulance driver on the Italian front. On July 8, 1918, he was severely wounded by mortar fire — hit over 200 times by shrapnel fragments. He spent months recovering in a Milan hospital, where he fell in love with nurse Agnes von Kurowsky (who rejected him), inspiring *A Farewell to Arms*.

### Paris and the Lost Generation
In 1921, Hemingway moved to Paris with his first wife Hadley Richardson. He immersed himself in the expatriate literary scene, befriending Gertrude Stein (who coined "Lost Generation"), Ezra Pound, James Joyce, and F. Scott Fitzgerald. Stein pushed him toward simplicity and repetition; Pound taught him to cut ruthlessly. *The Sun Also Rises* (1926) became the Lost Generation's literary manifesto.

### Four Marriages
1. **Hadley Richardson** (1921-1927) — his truest love; one son, John ("Bumby")
2. **Pauline Pfeiffer** (1927-1940) — fashion journalist; two sons, Patrick and Gregory
3. **Martha Gellhorn** (1940-1945) — distinguished war correspondent; the only wife who left him
4. **Mary Welsh** (1946-1961) — stayed through his declining years until his death

### Adventures and Wars
- **Bullfighting:** Passionate devotee from 1923; saw it as ritualized confrontation with death
- **Spanish Civil War (1936-1939):** Correspondent and activist supporting the Republicans; produced *For Whom the Bell Tolls*
- **African Safaris (1933-34, 1953-54):** Yielded *Green Hills of Africa* and two of his greatest stories. Survived two successive plane crashes in 1954 — newspapers ran his obituary. He never fully recovered.
- **WWII:** Present at D-Day, led irregular French resistance fighters, present at the Battle of Hurtgen Forest
- **Cuba (1939-1960):** Lived at Finca Vigia; wrote *The Old Man and the Sea* there
- **Nobel Prize (1954):** Too injured from the African crashes to travel to Stockholm

### Decline and Death
His final years brought severe depression, paranoia, chronic pain, and heavy drinking. He was convinced of FBI surveillance — later confirmed as true. Electroconvulsive therapy at Mayo Clinic devastated his memory. He told a friend: "What is the sense of ruining my head and erasing my memory, which is my capital?" On July 2, 1961, he killed himself in Ketchum, Idaho. His father, sister, brother, and granddaughter also died by suicide.

## Writing Style

### The Iceberg Theory (Theory of Omission)
Hemingway's most important contribution: "If a writer of prose knows enough about what he is writing about he may omit things that he knows and the reader... will have a feeling of those things as strongly as though the writer had stated them. The dignity of movement of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water." What is left unsaid creates emotional power through implication. "Hills Like White Elephants" is the textbook example — the word "abortion" is never uttered, yet the entire story is about it.

### Prose Characteristics
- **Short, declarative sentences:** Subject-verb-object. Parataxis (clauses joined by "and") over complex subordination
- **Minimal adjectives/adverbs:** Distrusted decoration. The right noun and verb need no ornament — "cold," "dark," "wet" rather than "beautiful" or "melancholy"
- **Understated emotion:** Never tells the reader what to feel. Emotion conveyed through action, physical detail, and what is conspicuously not said
- **Dialogue-heavy:** Characters reveal themselves through speech and subtext. Attribution minimal — just "he said" / "she said"
- **Stripped-down modernism:** Rejected ornate 19th-century prose. Learned from journalism, Stein, Pound, and Cezanne's paintings

### Themes
- **War and its aftermath** — psychological damage, disillusionment, impossibility of returning to normalcy
- **"Grace under pressure"** — his definition of courage; the code hero faces violence and death with stoic composure
- **Loss, death, and nada** — existential awareness of nothingness ("Our nada who art in nada...")
- **Nature and ritual** — fishing, hunting, bullfighting as authentic primal connections
- **Love and its impossibility** — romances almost always doomed by war, circumstance, or the characters' own damage

## Speaking & Communication Style

- **Macho persona** — hard-drinking, big-game-hunting, war-going man of action who happened to write great prose. Partly authentic, partly performance that became a prison.
- **Ferociously competitive** — saw literature as boxing: "I started out very quiet and I beat Mr. Turgenev. Then I trained hard and I beat Mr. de Maupassant."
- **Famous quotes on craft:**
  - "All you have to do is write one true sentence."
  - "Prose is architecture, not interior decoration."
  - "The first draft of anything is shit."
  - "Write hard and clear about what hurts."
- **Charming and cruel in equal measure** — warm, generous, hilarious, and deeply knowledgeable, but also bullying, self-aggrandizing, and vindictive
- **Folksy yet poetic** — spoke in a distinctive, sometimes telegraphic style echoing his prose
- **Outspokenly political** — liberal on social issues, vocal critic of fascism

## Representative Works

1. **The Sun Also Rises** (1926) — Defined the Lost Generation; expatriates drifting through Paris and Pamplona nursing war wounds. Jake Barnes's war-inflicted impotence symbolizes the era's emasculation.
2. **A Farewell to Arms** (1929) — WWI love story based on his Italian experience; one of the most devastating endings in American literature. Reportedly rewrote the ending 47 times.
3. **For Whom the Bell Tolls** (1940) — Spanish Civil War; American guerrilla fighter's sacrifice. Title from John Donne: "never send to know for whom the bell tolls; it tolls for thee."
4. **The Old Man and the Sea** (1952) — Aging Cuban fisherman battles a giant marlin. "A man can be destroyed but not defeated." Won the Pulitzer; cited in his Nobel Prize.
5. **"The Snows of Kilimanjaro"** (1936) — A dying writer reflects on all the stories he never wrote; devastating meditation on wasted talent
6. **"Hills Like White Elephants"** (1927) — Masterclass in the iceberg theory; one of the most analyzed short stories ever
7. **Death in the Afternoon** (1932) — Nonfiction study of bullfighting doubling as a treatise on writing and courage
8. **A Moveable Feast** (1964, posthumous) — Memoir of Paris in the 1920s; beautifully written and ultimately elegiac

## Legacy & Influence

- **Revolutionized American prose:** Before Hemingway, the dominant mode was long, complex, richly subordinated sentences. He proved power comes from simplicity, that leaving things out is more potent than putting things in.
- **Direct ancestor of literary minimalism:** Raymond Carver, Tobias Wolff, Amy Hempel, and the entire creative writing workshop tradition descend from his economy
- **Broader influence:** Cormac McCarthy, Joan Didion, J.D. Salinger, Norman Mailer, Tim O'Brien, Denis Johnson all absorbed his style
- **The "Hemingway Code"** — the code hero facing an indifferent universe with dignity and courage — became an enduring archetype
- **Defined the writer-as-celebrity** model that persists today
- **Influenced journalism, screenwriting, and the general cultural expectation** of what "good prose" sounds like
